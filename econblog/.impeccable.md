## Design Context

### Users
Adults (20-45) who want to genuinely understand economics — not hobbyists, not tourists. They're paying $19.99/month or $499 lifetime because they take this seriously. They sit at desks, on laptops, during focused study sessions. Desktop-primary audience. They've likely tried Coursera or Khan Academy and found it either too boring or too shallow. They want density and rigor without the institutional ugliness of a university LMS.

### Brand Personality
**Sharp, provocative, dense** — like The Economist meets a well-written textbook. This is not friendly-hand-holding education. It's content that respects the reader's intelligence, challenges them with real consequences (XP penalties, lockouts), and rewards genuine understanding. The tone is confident without being cold. There's warmth, but it comes from craft and care in the presentation, not from mascots or cheerful copy.

### Emotional Goals
**Earned competence.** When someone finishes a lesson, they should feel: "I actually understand this now, and I worked for it." The interface should make the work feel meaningful — not easy, not punishing, but worth it. Every quiz gate they pass should feel like progress they own.

### Aesthetic Direction
- **Reference:** Atlas Pilot — tabbed workspace structure, warm tones, structured information hierarchy, cards with purpose
- **Theme:** Light. Clean white backgrounds (barely tinted cool toward blue, not warm/cream). Dense reading content demands high contrast and clean surfaces for sustained focus.
- **Color anchors:** Blue for primary actions and active states. Gold/amber for XP, achievements, mastery — the reward color. Neutrals tinted warm toward the brand. No purple. No neon. No gradients on text.
- **Typography:** Dense, editorial. Needs a display face with personality (not generic sans-serif) paired with a legible body face for long-form reading. The type should feel like a well-designed magazine or journal, not a SaaS dashboard.
- **Spacing:** Generous but purposeful. The content is dense — whitespace should provide relief, not padding. Varied spacing creates hierarchy (tight within groups, generous between sections).
- **Motion:** Minimal and purposeful. Subtle entrance animations on page load. No bounce, no elastic. Exponential easing only. Respect `prefers-reduced-motion`.

### Anti-References
- **Duolingo:** Too gamified, too childish, too many mascots and celebrations. Our XP system has teeth (penalties, lockouts) — it's not a game.
- **Coursera/edX:** Too institutional, too boring, feels like a university LMS wrapped in a slightly nicer skin. No soul.
- **Crypto dashboards:** Dark mode glow, neon gradients, purple-blue schemes, glassmorphism. The opposite of what we want.

### Design Principles
1. **Density with clarity.** Pack information tight but make hierarchy unmistakable. Every heading level, every spacing choice, every color use should signal structure.
2. **Warmth through craft.** No emoji, no mascots, no illustrations. Warmth comes from color temperature, typography quality, and spacing rhythm. The care is in the details.
3. **Earned, not given.** The UI should reflect the learning model — progress is visible, gates are clear, achievements feel substantial. Nothing should feel cheap or automatic.
4. **Editorial over dashboard.** This is closer to a well-designed publication than a SaaS product. Content is king. Chrome gets out of the way.
5. **No AI tells.** No gradient text, no side-stripe borders, no card-in-card nesting, no glassmorphism, no hero metric templates, no generic rounded-rectangle-with-shadow cards.
